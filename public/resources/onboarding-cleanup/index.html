Guide

How to clean up onboarding when your IT team is overwhelmed.

Onboarding breaks quietly. One extra manual step turns into ten, tool ownership gets fuzzy, and new hires feel the friction before anyone has time to fix the process. Start with the pieces that make the next hire easier.

Signs onboarding is breaking

Look for friction, rework, and hidden ownership.

A messy onboarding process does not always look dramatic. It often looks like repeated Slack messages, managers asking who approves a tool, IT creating accounts from memory, or new hires waiting on access during their first week.

  • New hires do not have the right access on day one.
  • Managers, HR, IT, and Finance each own part of the process, but no one owns the full path.
  • Access requests are split across Slack, Jira, email, spreadsheets, and direct messages.
  • Offboarding checklists exist, but onboarding is mostly copied from the last person.
  • People rely on one experienced teammate to remember the exceptions.

Common causes

The problem is usually a system problem, not a motivation problem.

Most teams are not careless. They are moving quickly, adding tools, changing managers, and trying to keep the business running. Onboarding gets messy when the workflow grows faster than the documentation and ownership model.

  • Role-based access is not defined, so each hire becomes a custom project.
  • Tool ownership is unclear, especially for SaaS apps owned outside IT.
  • There is no single source of truth for start dates, department, manager, equipment, and access.
  • Approvals happen informally, which makes them hard to track or improve.
  • Automation was added around an unclear process, so it saves less time than expected.

Document first

Start with the parts people repeat every time.

Before buying another tool or building a complex workflow, write down what already happens. Keep it boring and specific. The goal is to make the process visible enough that someone else can follow it and spot where it breaks.

1. Define the trigger

What tells IT that a new hire is coming? Include the source, timing, and required fields.

2. List the baseline access

Write down what every employee needs, then what changes by department, role, location, or manager.

3. Name each owner

For every app, device, group, approval, and exception, name the person or team responsible.

4. Capture the handoffs

Document what HR, IT, Finance, Security, managers, and office teams need from each other.

Where automation helps

Automate the repeatable parts after the workflow is clear.

Automation is most useful when the inputs, approvals, and owners are already clear. Start with small wins that reduce repeated manual work and make exceptions easier to see.

  • Use HRIS or form data to trigger a standard onboarding ticket or task list.
  • Create templates for common roles instead of rebuilding access from memory.
  • Route approvals to the right manager or app owner instead of asking in open channels.
  • Use groups where possible so access can be added and removed consistently.
  • Track exceptions separately so they do not quietly become the new default.

When to bring in outside help

Bring in support when the cleanup needs momentum or neutral structure.

You may be able to clean up onboarding internally if the team has time, ownership, and enough context. Outside help can be useful when the work keeps getting postponed, IT headcount has been reduced, or the process crosses teams that need someone to map the workflow without getting stuck in old habits.

  • The team knows onboarding is messy but no one has time to untangle it.
  • Multiple teams disagree about who owns which step.
  • Access and identity workflows need cleanup before automation will help.
  • You need practical documentation, not a giant strategy deck.
  • You want someone who understands both the technical side and the people/process side of IT.
